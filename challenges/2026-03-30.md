# Enforcing Supply Chain Integrity with SLSA Provenance and Sigstore

**Date:** 2026-03-30
**Topic:** DevSecOps
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Estimated Time:** 1–1.5 hours

## Scenario

Your organization recently adopted a GitLab CI/CD pipeline to build and ship a containerized microservice. A post-incident review following a high-profile supply chain attack (similar to the XZ Utils backdoor) revealed that your pipeline produces artifacts with no verifiable provenance — anyone with CI write access (or who compromises a runner) could substitute a malicious image and the downstream Kubernetes cluster would pull and run it without question.

You've been tasked with hardening the pipeline. The security team has mandated SLSA Build Level 2 compliance: all container images must be signed with Sigstore's `cosign` (keyless, OIDC-based), and a machine-verifiable SLSA provenance attestation must accompany every image pushed to the registry. A follow-on policy must prevent unsigned or unattested images from being deployed to the staging namespace.

Your environment: GitLab CI with a project-level OIDC token, a container registry (assume `registry.gitlab.com/<your-group>/<your-project>`), and a staging Kubernetes cluster with Kyverno as the admission controller.

## Objectives

- [ ] Configure a GitLab CI job that uses `cosign sign` (keyless) to sign a built container image using the GitLab OIDC token, binding identity to the pipeline's OIDC claims.
- [ ] Generate and attach a SLSA provenance attestation to the image using `cosign attest` with a CycloneDX or SLSA predicate, and verify it locally with `cosign verify-attestation`.
- [ ] Write a Kyverno `ClusterPolicy` that blocks pod admission in the `staging` namespace unless the image has a valid Sigstore signature from your GitLab CI OIDC issuer.
- [ ] Identify at least two ways an attacker could still bypass this control and document mitigations for each.

## Hints

1. GitLab CI exposes `CI_JOB_JWT_V2` (or the newer OIDC token via `id_tokens`) for keyless signing — look at how `cosign` uses `SIGSTORE_ID_TOKEN` or the `--identity-token` flag to authenticate against Fulcio without managing private keys.
2. For the Kyverno policy, the `verifyImages` rule type is what you want. Pay close attention to the `attestors` block and how you specify the OIDC issuer (`https://gitlab.com`) and subject pattern (`https://gitlab.com/<your-group>/<your-project>//.gitlab-ci.yml@refs/heads/main`) to pin verification to your specific pipeline.
3. Two common bypasses: (a) an attacker with `developer` role can push a branch that triggers CI and signs an image under your OIDC subject pattern — consider what branch-ref pinning in the Kyverno policy actually enforces; (b) Rekor's transparency log is append-only but not a policy engine — a signed image from a compromised earlier pipeline job is still "valid." Think about how timestamp/epoch-based policies or signature revocation (via Rekor search) would help.

## Review Questions

1. Keyless signing ties a signature to an OIDC identity claim (issuer + subject). What are the trust assumptions this model rests on, and how does Rekor's transparency log reduce — but not eliminate — the risk of a compromised OIDC provider?
2. SLSA Build Level 2 requires a "hosted" build platform and machine-generated provenance, but does not require hermetic builds. In a GitLab shared-runner environment, what concrete attack surface remains that SLSA Level 3's hermetic build requirement would close?
3. How would you extend this control to cover third-party base images pulled in your `FROM` statements — where you have no ability to re-sign the upstream image — without blocking your builds or creating an unmanageable allowlist?
